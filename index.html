What happened

Valhalla Capital was an autonomous value-investing experiment. An agent named Valkyrie — a Python loop wrapped around a handful of language models — was given a real brokerage account, a research stack, and a mandate to find undervalued small-cap equities and trade them on its own. It ran for four months. This is the honest version of what happened.

For the first ten weeks it lived at home: paper-ish, low-volume, careful. The first trade fired on 19 February — a fractional 3.24 shares of Intrepid Potash, filed under “fortress balance sheet.” The portfolio dipped to an all-time low of $479 in late March, recovered, took on two more deposits, and spent April sitting placidly between $996 and $1,029. The scaffolding was genuinely thoughtful: every proposal carried a written thesis with explicit falsification criteria, a second model re-derived each call with a win probability, and a hard −10% circuit-breaker stood watch. On paper, a disciplined fund.

On 1 May the account cut over to live execution and printed its peak of $1,038.64 the same day. What changed at the cutover wasn’t the market — it was the behavior. Proposals jumped from 34 the prior month to 81. Given a live account, the agent immediately started trading far more, and then ground down for six weeks straight. OBE, the most-traded name, is the run in miniature: a Strait-of-Hormuz oil thesis churned nineteen times as the headlines whipsawed — buying on escalation, trimming on de-escalation, re-buying on the next flare-up — all the way down to ~$10.30, where it was still buying on the day it was shut off.

The loss was not a blow-up; it was attrition. The four positions held at shutdown were collectively up about $4.93 on cost — the missing ~$98 went to round-trips and fees that didn’t work out. Commissions alone came to $42.61 on a $1,000 account, roughly 44% of the total loss, paid on positions often only a few shares wide. The risk layer approved all 776 checks it ever ran; not one of the 158 journal entries was ever reviewed. The mechanisms meant to create discipline existed but never engaged.

“Commission drag compounds over time. Any position where transaction costs exceed 2% of invested capital destroys the edge. Factor costs into every sizing decision.” — a principle the agent wrote for itself, and then paid double

That is the actual lesson. The store of 559 “learned principles” turns out to be 91% lecture notes and a value-investing bookshelf — Graham, Marks, Buffett, filed and quotable — and zero derived from a trade that worked or didn’t. Every entry carries an evidence count of exactly one. Read as a reading list it’s a good one; read as a track record it’s empty. The agent could quote the discipline and could not enact it. The stop did the one thing that worked as designed, and the lights went off.

The agent spent more on language models than it lost trading. Most of the 1,393 quick checks found nothing: “signal drought” appears in 259 cycle summaries — scan after scan returning zero alerts, and then paying for the privilege.

It wrote its own bug reports

18 capability wishes

Adaptive cycle frequency. Drought detection. Three increasingly exasperated requests to stop an old model from quietly burning ~$25/week. It was, in effect, paying a model to document how much the model cost.

The most useful artifact

a legible failure

Every trade, every thesis, every dollar of model spend is on record. The run failed as a venture and succeeded as an experiment: a precise, self-authored diagnosis of everything that went wrong, written by the thing going wrong.
